Switzerland requirements at a glance

Swiss licensing requires a Swiss entity, Swiss substance (board, compliance officer), and tier-appropriate capital and operational framework.

  • Swiss entity: Swiss AG (joint-stock) or GmbH (limited liability). Mandatory.
  • Paid-up capital (AG): CHF 100,000 minimum capital, CHF 50,000 paid-up. FINMA Fintech requires CHF 300,000 paid-up.
  • Swiss-resident director: At least 1 Swiss-resident director or representative with signing authority. Mandatory under Swiss company law.
  • Swiss MLRO: Compliance officer based in or regularly available in Switzerland. Recognized AML credential (CAMS, ICA).
  • AML program: Swiss AML Act + FINMA / VQF rules compliant. Documented procedures, risk assessment, training.
  • Audit: Annual audit by Swiss-recognized auditor (BDO, KPMG, EY, Deloitte, PwC, or specialized smaller firms).
  • Capital adequacy ongoing: Capital must be maintained throughout operations. FINMA Fintech reports quarterly to FINMA.

Glossary: key terms for Switzerland crypto setup

If you're new to crypto licensing in Switzerland, these are the terms you'll encounter most often.

FINMA
Swiss Financial Market Supervisory Authority — federal regulator overseeing banks, insurers, securities dealers, and certain crypto activities.
VQF
Verein zur Qualitätssicherung von Finanzdienstleistungen — a FINMA-recognized Self-Regulatory Organization (SRO) for financial intermediaries including crypto operators.
DLT Act
Distributed Ledger Technology Act (2021) — Swiss law creating regulatory framework for tokenized securities and DLT trading facilities.
FINMA Fintech License
License for fintech firms accepting public deposits up to CHF 100 million. Lighter than full banking license; suitable for crypto exchanges and custody.
Swiss AG
Aktiengesellschaft — Swiss joint-stock company. CHF 100K minimum capital, CHF 50K paid-up. Standard for FINMA-licensed entities.
Crypto Valley
Zug canton's crypto cluster — 1,000+ blockchain companies including Ethereum Foundation, Cardano Foundation, Tezos, Solana.
SRO Membership
Self-Regulatory Organization membership — alternative to direct FINMA supervision for financial intermediaries below banking threshold.
Lex Koller
Swiss law restricting foreign property ownership. Doesn't directly affect crypto licensing but relevant for foreign-owned Swiss entity setups.

Switzerland crypto license — frequently asked questions

The most common questions from founders considering Switzerland for their crypto company in 2026.

How much does a Swiss crypto license cost in 2026?

Three tiers: VQF SRO membership $30K–$80K (cheapest), FINMA Fintech license $150K–$350K (medium), DLT/Banking license $400K+ (highest tier). VQF is the most common pathway for crypto exchanges and brokers; Fintech is for operators accepting public deposits up to CHF 100M.

VQF vs FINMA Fintech — which should I choose?

VQF SRO membership suits crypto operators acting as financial intermediaries (exchanges, brokers, OTC) without taking public deposits. Faster, cheaper, FINMA-recognized but not directly FINMA-supervised. FINMA Fintech license required if you accept public deposits or want direct FINMA supervision (institutional credibility, EU acceptance). Most crypto operators start with VQF and upgrade if growth demands.

Does Switzerland provide an EU passport for crypto?

No. Switzerland is not in the EU/EEA, so Swiss crypto licenses don't grant MiCA passport. Swiss-licensed entities serving EU customers must additionally comply with MiCA (typically by setting up a separate EU CASP entity in Lithuania, Cyprus, etc.). For EU-only target market, Lithuania CASP is the cost-effective choice.

What is the Swiss DLT Act?

The Distributed Ledger Technology Act (effective 2021) is Switzerland's purpose-built crypto law. It creates a new license category — DLT trading facility — for tokenized securities exchanges. It also clarifies the legal status of digital assets, segregation in bankruptcy, and DLT-based registers. Switzerland was an early mover globally with this dedicated framework.

Why is Crypto Valley (Zug) significant?

Zug is home to 1,000+ crypto and blockchain companies, including Ethereum Foundation, Cardano Foundation, Tezos Foundation, Solana, Polkadot Foundation. Strong network effects, deep blockchain talent pool, crypto-friendly local government, and proximity to Sygnum/SEBA crypto banks. Many Swiss crypto applicants choose Zug as their base.

How long does Swiss crypto licensing take?

VQF SRO: 2–4 months end-to-end. FINMA Fintech license: 6–12 months. DLT trading facility / Banking license: 12+ months. The Swiss entity formation is fast (2–4 weeks); the regulatory review is the bottleneck.
